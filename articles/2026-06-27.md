On June 25, 2026, the fact-checking organization Rappler confirmed what should have been obvious: digitally manipulated photos showing political figures Kiko Pangilinan and Risa Hontiveros with the suspects in the Tacloban school shooting were [categorically false](https://www.rappler.com/newsbreak/fact-check/kiko-pangilinan-risa-hontiveros-tacloban-city-school-shooting-suspects-photos/). Two days prior, the same outlet had published an article on how the shooting revived [fears of online extremism](httpss://www.rappler.com/technology/tacloban-school-shooting-fears-online-violent-extremism/). The sequence is telling. First the tragedy, then the analysis of the digital fallout, and finally, the clean-up of a deliberate, targeted lie.

This incident, however, is not merely another case of “disinformation,” as the prevailing and bloodless frame suggests. It is a calculated act of narrative warfare, a deliberate exploitation of national trauma to assassinate character and erode public trust in established institutions. To label this simply as the spread of unverified claims is to willfully ignore the malice of its architecture. This was not a mistake; it was an attack.

The pattern is now grimly familiar. A real-world event creates a vacuum of information and a surplus of emotion. Into this void, malicious actors inject a payload of weaponized falsehood. In this case, the payload was a set of doctored images designed for maximum emotional impact, linking opposition figures to an abhorrent crime. The goal is not to convince the critical observer, but to inflame the partisan, to sow doubt among the undecided, and to permanently stain the reputations of political enemies. The truth, when it eventually arrives via a fact-check, is a quiet medic appearing on a battlefield long after the bombs have fallen. The damage is already done, the narrative poison already injected into the digital bloodstream.

The response from authorities reveals two competing, and ultimately inadequate, understandings of the threat. Rappler, by fact-checking, treats the problem as one of information integrity. Their work is necessary, but it is a fundamentally defensive and reactive posture. It is the digital equivalent of defusing a single bomb while the factory down the road produces a thousand more. The frame of “disinformation targeting political figures” is precise but fails to capture the strategic nature of the assault. It is not a prank. It is not random noise. It is a low-cost, high-impact method of political sabotage.

A more grounded, if incomplete, diagnosis comes from Tacloban City Mayor Alfred Romualdez. On [June 25](https://x.com/bncdotph/status/2069784933728649534), he correctly identified the core issue as a breakdown of order, warning that unrestricted access to online content exposes minors to harmful influences. His support for age limits on social media is a step, however tentative, toward acknowledging that the digital sphere is not a self-regulating utopia but an ungoverned territory where predators roam.

Mayor Romualdez’s focus on the vulnerability of minors is laudable, but the problem is far broader. The entire body politic is now vulnerable. The same mechanisms that expose children to harm are being used to expose the nation to strategic destabilization. His call for regulation, for imposing rules and asserting authority, is the only coherent path forward. It recognizes that order and the rule of law must extend to the digital domain. This is not a matter of censorship, but of national sovereignty. A nation that cannot control the flow of strategic attacks within its own information space is a nation that is no longer fully sovereign.

The current approach—letting chaos reign and then sending in fact-checkers to sweep up the debris—is a recipe for failure. It cedes the initiative entirely to those who wish to tear down institutions. It allows anonymous actors to hijack a public tragedy and use it as a political cudgel, with no consequences. This is a direct challenge to the authority of the state and the very concept of a shared, fact-based reality. The spread of these fakes is not an issue of free speech; it is an act of sedition committed with pixels instead of bullets.

The conversation must therefore shift. We must move beyond the sterile language of “fact-checking” and “misinformation” and begin speaking in the language of national security, narrative defense, and institutional integrity. The perpetrators of such acts are not mere trolls; they are combatants in a new kind of war. Their actions are not “engagement farming”; they are calculated attacks on social cohesion and the rule of law.

These digital ambushes will become the standard operating procedure following any national crisis. They will grow more sophisticated, more personalized, and more destructive. Unless the state reclaims its authority over the digital domain, treating these acts not as speech but as strategic attacks on national stability, our shared reality will be the next casualty.
