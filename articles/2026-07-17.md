On May 9, 2026, Pastor Mark Burns dedicated a gold statue of Donald Trump, an act that RT_com and @ProudSocialist framed as an "anti-idolatry gesture" while simultaneously asserting that Trump understands the Bible better than Pope Leo. Eight days later, an Israeli Arab commentator appeared on social media [criticizing anti-Israel sentiment](https://x.com/VividProwess/status/2058374927817027798) by invoking biblical references about Arabs as colonizers and Jesus being born in a Jewish city. These two events, separated by little more than a week, reveal a pattern that demands examination: the weaponization of religious rhetoric to deflect accountability and manufacture tribal allegiance.

The claim is this: religious narratives are being systematically deployed not as expressions of faith but as tactical infrastructure to shield political actors from substantive scrutiny.

Consider the gold statue dedication. The explicit biblical commandment against worshipping false idols could not be clearer. Yet the framing presented by RT_com transforms this act into its opposite—an anti-idolatry gesture. This inversion requires the audience to abandon textual literalism in favor of tribal loyalty. The supporting claim that Trump "understands the Bible better than Pope Leo" while having "thwarted Iran's weapons program" reveals the mechanism: religious authority is granted not through theological scholarship or ecclesiastical tradition but through political utility. The Bible becomes a prop in a larger performance where the text means whatever serves the immediate political objective.

The Israeli Arab commentary on May 17 operates within the same tactical framework. By citing biblical references to assert that Arabs are colonizers and that Jesus was born in a Jewish city, the argument constructs a historical narrative designed to delegitimize contemporary political claims. The specific framing—that anti-Israel sentiment can be dismissed through scriptural archaeology—transforms religious text into territorial deed. This is not exegesis; it is strategic narrative construction employing the weight of sacred text to foreclose political debate.

What connects these events is not theological coherence but operational similarity. Both deploy religious rhetoric to achieve political ends. Both rely on emotional appeals rather than verifiable claims. Both create tribal signals that separate audiences into believers and enemies. The content differs; the machinery is identical.

The competing frame offered in this discourse—"Procedural Theater vs. Real Accountability"—deserves direct engagement. Various governments and institutions, the brief notes, produce procedural announcements without substantive consequences. Reopened files, Ombudsman investigations—these create the appearance of accountability while delivering none. This frame challenges my read by suggesting the problem is institutional rather than rhetorical. Perhaps the gold statues and biblical deflections are merely symptoms of a deeper failure: systems that perform oversight without executing it.

I do not accept this frame as superior, though I acknowledge its partial truth. Procedural theater and rhetorical manipulation are not competing explanations—they are complementary tactics. The institutions produce announcements that occupy attention. The religious rhetoric provides emotional infrastructure that makes audiences indifferent to whether those announcements produce results. A population conditioned to evaluate claims through tribal loyalty rather than evidentiary standards will not demand accountability from institutions aligned with their tribe. The procedural theater works precisely because the rhetorical manipulation has already prepared the ground.

The [May 8 criticism](https://x.com/atrupar/status/2052390460425928874) of the 30% of Americans opposing Pope Leo's peace urging demonstrates this dynamic. The framing positions opposition to papal peace advocacy as virtuous—a position that requires subordinating the Pope's religious authority to political preference. When religious authority itself becomes contingent on political alignment, no institution retains the standing to demand accountability.

Order and rule of law cannot function when the foundational texts meant to provide moral grounding become infinitely malleable. The commandment against idolatry means nothing if a gold statue can be reframed as its opposite. Historical claims about biblical geography mean nothing if they exist only to service contemporary political disputes. What remains is pure power: whoever controls the interpretation controls the audience, and whoever controls the audience faces no accountability.

National sovereignty and the preservation of national identity—values I hold—require institutions capable of enforcing boundaries and maintaining coherence. Those institutions cannot survive in an environment where truth itself has become tribal property. The same mechanism that transforms a gold statue into an anti-idolatry gesture can transform any law into its opposite, any investigation into exoneration, any accountability into persecution.

The consequence is predictable and arriving. As religious rhetoric becomes fully instrumentalized, the audiences conditioned by it will prove incapable of recognizing genuine threats to the values they claim to defend. The next gold statue will require less justification. The next biblical deflection will face less scrutiny. The institutions producing procedural theater will face even less pressure to deliver substance, because the populations they nominally serve will have lost the vocabulary to demand it.

What must happen is specific: religious communities that recognize this pattern must name it publicly, at cost to their political alliances. The alternative is watching their sacred texts become permanent props in a performance that serves neither faith nor nation—only the actors who have learned to manipulate both.
