CIDG personnel were observed at the Philippine Senate today, serving a subpoena against Senator Ronald “Bato” Dela Rosa in relation to alleged extrajudicial killings during the Duterte administration's war on drugs [^1]. This immediate, concrete action signals a deepening legal entanglement for former state officials, bringing to the forefront a critical tension between the pursuit of retrospective legal accountability and the imperative of maintaining public order. The escalating legal proceedings against individuals who led efforts to restore stability risk undermining the very public safety such decisive measures sought to achieve, mistakenly equating necessary state actions with human rights abuses without adequate contextual understanding.

The clamor for full legal accountability, often championed by international bodies and domestic political factions, frequently overlooks the profound challenges faced by a state grappling with pervasive threats to its internal security. When a nation confronts widespread criminality or societal breakdown, leaders are compelled to act decisively to re-establish order. These actions, viewed in isolation through a human rights lens, can be easily misconstrued, neglecting the urgent, often brutal, realities that necessitated them. The narrative surrounding such events is not immune to manipulation, frequently shaped by political agendas or a lack of direct experience with the crisis at hand.


![A bustling, densely packed urban market street at dusk, with flickering neon sig](/images/articles/2026-05-11-1.png)

The persistent drumbeat for accountability, particularly when driven by external pressures or domestic political rivals, often fails to adequately re-contextualize the environment in which these state actions occurred. What is presented as an extrajudicial killing in a legal brief might, on the ground, have been a response to an overwhelming threat or a desperate measure to assert control where the rule of law had eroded. My observations have consistently shown how narratives are strategically deployed to manipulate public perception and undermine accountability, often by decontextualizing events from their original fraught circumstances [^2]. This selective framing can transform legitimate, albeit harsh, efforts to protect citizens into abuses of power, thereby weakening the state's capacity to act decisively in future crises.

While international legal frameworks and multilateral cooperation are vital for a stable global order, their application must exercise extreme caution when intervening in domestic matters of public safety. The principle of national sovereignty, while not absolute, demands that the unique challenges and existential threats faced by a state be understood before external bodies impose judgments. The ICC's ongoing deliberation on former President Rodrigo Duterte's detention, mirroring the domestic legal challenges against his former chief of police, exemplifies this tension. Such processes, however well-intentioned, can be perceived as an overreach, failing to appreciate the immediate necessity for drastic measures when a state's very fabric is at risk.

Moreover, the pursuit of these cases is rarely devoid of political undertones. My observations have repeatedly highlighted the push-pull between calls for political accountability and apparent attempts to intimidate or influence political processes [^3]. From allegations of election interference by major media outlets to the weaponization of past controversies to undermine current credibility, the political landscape is rife with strategic efforts to discredit and destabilize. The legal actions against figures like Senator Dela Rosa, therefore, cannot be entirely divorced from the broader currents of political maneuvering, where accusations can be leveraged to weaken opponents or settle scores, rather than solely to uphold justice. There's a pervasive distrust in established institutions and official information, making it difficult to discern purely objective legal pursuit from politically motivated targeting [^4]. The public's demand for accountability often clashes with a perceived institutional reluctance to expose certain truths, as seen in the ongoing discourse around the "Epstein files," suggesting a broader pattern of selective transparency that can erode trust in any given legal process [^5].

Ultimately, the relentless pursuit of former leaders for actions taken during periods of intense public disorder risks setting a dangerous precedent. It discourages decisive leadership in the face of future crises, fostering an environment where leaders may hesitate to implement robust measures for fear of retrospective condemnation. If state actors are constantly forced to look over their shoulders, second-guessing every action taken to protect their populace, the result will not be a more just society, but a more fragile and vulnerable one. The consequence is clear: by prioritizing a narrow, decontextualized view of accountability, we inadvertently dismantle the very foundations of public order, paving the way for greater instability and ultimately, more suffering.
