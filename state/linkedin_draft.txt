I've been following the impeachment proceedings against Vice President Sara Duterte in the Philippines, and one detail keeps surfacing that speaks to a broader pattern in how institutional processes get co-opted.

According to multiple reports from ABS-CBN and local journalists, security measures around the trial venue aren't just about safety—they're calibrated to amplify local support for the Vice President. What's presented as procedural necessity becomes a stage for political messaging. This is strategic narrative construction in real time: the form of accountability (an impeachment trial) is preserved, while the substance is quietly redirected.

This isn't unique to the Philippines. I've tracked similar dynamics in the U.S., Brazil, and across Europe—moments where the architecture of oversight remains intact but the outcome is pre-negotiated through spectacle, controlled access, and messaging that frames the accused as besieged rather than accountable. The mechanism is always the same: use the legitimacy of the institution to confer legitimacy on the actor, while ensuring the process can't reach an inconvenient conclusion.

For those of us working in governance, communications, or institutional integrity: when does heightened security around a legal process stop being protective and start being performative? What are the markers that distinguish genuine accountability from its theatrical simulation?
