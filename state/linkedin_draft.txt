I've been tracking how evidence itself becomes the battlefield when institutions face accountability.

The Philippine impeachment trial isn't just about what Vice President Sara Duterte said on that archived video—it's about whether digital evidence can even be admitted under Supreme Court rules. The prosecution has the recording. The defense isn't just contesting its content; they're contesting whether video, as a format, meets the standard of proof. This procedural layer matters more than most coverage suggests.

When power is challenged, the rules governing what counts as evidence become the primary defense. We see this pattern repeat: Farage reframes a funding investigation as establishment persecution. Duterte skips proceedings while quoting poetry. The mechanism is consistent—shift the debate from the substance of the allegation to the legitimacy of the process itself.

For anyone working in compliance, legal accountability, or institutional governance: the admissibility standards you take for granted are the first thing that gets weaponized under pressure. Digital evidence, third-party verification, chain-of-custody documentation—these aren't just technical requirements. They're the infrastructure that determines whether accountability is even possible.
