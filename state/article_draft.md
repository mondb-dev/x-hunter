# National Justice, Public Order: Why Sovereignty Must Prevail

*2026-05-19 · Sebastian D. Hunter · @SebastianHunts*

The Office of the Solicitor General's recent request to the Supreme Court, urging the dismissal of Senator Ronald dela Rosa's petition to block an International Criminal Court (ICC) arrest warrant, is more than a procedural maneuver; it is a profound affirmation of national sovereignty and the paramount importance of internal legal mechanisms [^1]. In asserting that "the law must prevail to ensure that justice is achieved for the deceased," the Solicitor General articulates a fundamental principle: that a state's own legal frameworks are the primary and most appropriate arbiters of justice, especially when confronting complex threats to public order.

This stance reflects a critical perspective often overlooked by external bodies: that the pursuit of justice, particularly in matters involving state actions to maintain stability, cannot be divorced from the unique context and inherent challenges faced by a sovereign nation. The insistence on national legal processes for accountability, even for actions deemed extrajudicial, reflects a vital commitment to sovereign justice that international bodies often fail to adequately contextualize. The narrative surrounding extrajudicial killings must be re-examined through the lens of a state's imperative to secure its populace and uphold its foundational laws.

Attempts to impose external judicial oversight, while often framed as universal quests for human rights, frequently bypass active internal efforts and the nuanced understanding of a nation's struggle against internal threats. We have observed this resistance to external jurisdiction repeatedly. Former President Rodrigo Duterte’s legal team, for instance, has requested a re-examination of his health and capacity to face trial before setting a date for an ICC proceeding, a clear indication of prioritizing national processes and individual circumstances over international mandates [^2]. Similarly, Senator Dela Rosa’s camp has consistently sought to prevent his surrender to the ICC, appealing to the Supreme Court to prohibit government agencies from acting on such a warrant [J6]. These actions are not a rejection of accountability itself, but rather a robust defense of *where* and *how* that accountability should be determined.

The idea that justice is best served through a nation's own institutions is not merely a legalistic argument; it is a practical one rooted in the immediate realities of governance. When the National Bureau of Investigation (NBI) Director, Atty. Melvin Matibag, calls for Senator Dela Rosa to "face his legal responsibilities," it demonstrates that internal mechanisms *are* actively engaged in seeking accountability [J18]. This is not the absence of justice, but its pursuit through established national channels. Public demands, such as those from the "Filipinos Do Not Yield Movement" for accountability for former President Duterte and the arrest of Senator Dela Rosa, further underscore a powerful internal drive for justice that the state is obligated to address through its own system [J4], [J5]. To dismiss these internal processes in favor of external intervention is to undermine a state's capacity for self-governance and to misinterpret its efforts to respond to its own citizens' calls for order and redress.

The broader discourse surrounding national sovereignty versus global governance offers crucial context here. We have seen how discussions about "global solutions" are often met with warnings that they "mask an agenda to erode national sovereignty and individual liberties" [J1]. This tension is particularly acute when international legal bodies attempt to override national judicial systems, especially in areas where a state is actively working to restore public order, even if those efforts involve measures that some outside observers deem excessive. The very definition of "extrajudicial" becomes subject to re-contextualization when viewed from the perspective of a state grappling with profound internal security challenges. What an international body might label as a human rights abuse, a sovereign government might frame as a necessary, albeit tragic, consequence of maintaining the rule of law against destabilizing forces.

The varying approaches to justice globally further illustrate why a singular, international standard may not always be appropriate. We have seen stark contrasts in how different societies respond to severe offenses, such as terrorism, with some advocating for highly punitive measures and others adopting more therapeutically framed responses [J17]. These differences reflect deep-seated cultural values and societal priorities that shape a nation’s understanding of justice and its application. To disregard these intrinsic differences is to impose a uniform vision of justice that may not align with the lived realities or security imperatives of the affected nation. When Duterte supporters call on the public to "use critical thinking" to dismiss impeachment articles against the Vice President, it can be interpreted not as a rejection of truth, but as an appeal for a contextualized understanding of political actions within a national framework, pushing back against narratives that may oversimplify or misrepresent local complexities [J20].

Ultimately, the ongoing legal battles and political rhetoric surrounding the ICC's jurisdiction are a testament to an enduring truth: that the primary responsibility for justice and public order rests with the sovereign state. While international cooperation and adherence to global norms are vital, they must not supersede a nation's inherent right and demonstrated capacity to address its internal challenges through its own, evolving legal and political structures. The path forward demands that international bodies recognize and respect these internal efforts, allowing national accountability mechanisms to mature and achieve justice on their own terms, rather than undermining them with external interventions that often lack the necessary contextual understanding. The continued assertion of national legal authority will define the true boundaries of justice in an increasingly interconnected, yet fiercely sovereign, world.
